## 331-Unit Configuration for a 12 m Road Section
A Georgetown deployment built around **331 split-type solar streetlight units** is sized for a **12 m carriageway** with **21 m pole spacing** and **7 m poles**. The lighting head is specified at **100 W LED** with **15,000 lm** output, which places the system in the municipal corridor category rather than pedestrian-scale lighting. For procurement and design review, the key point is that this is a **split-type solar streetlight 200W panel** class installation in terms of energy architecture, even though the lamp head itself is **100 W**.

The arrangement is suitable for collector roads, port access roads, and urban links where trenching for grid supply is undesirable. The configuration assumes **dusk-to-dawn operation** supported by solar charging and battery storage, with the panel and battery separated from the luminaire body for easier thermal management and service access.

## Core Engineering Parameters
The pole structure is a **7 m hot-dip galvanized steel** mast rated for **45 m/s wind resistance** and a **25-year structural life**. Wiring is routed **inside the pole**, while the **battery box is mounted externally** on the pole body. That layout simplifies maintenance and keeps the battery compartment accessible without dismantling the full lighting head.

The solar module is specified as **1240 W Mono PERC**, with **21% efficiency**, **0.4%/yr degradation**, and a **25-year panel warranty**. Storage uses a **12 V / 250 Ah LiFePO4** battery pack with **3500 cycles**, **90% DoD**, and **160 Wh/kg** energy density. In practical terms, the battery is sized to provide **3–5 days** of cloudy-weather autonomy, which is the relevant metric for coastal operation where consecutive low-irradiance days can occur.

The control package combines **motion sensing**, **timer control**, and **4G/LoRa remote monitoring**. For a municipal operator, this means the fixture can be dimmed or scheduled by time window, while fault status can be checked remotely at the pole level instead of relying on manual night patrols.

### Standards and installation notes
The design basis references **IEC 60598**, **IEC 62124**, and **CJJ 45-2015**. These standards are relevant to luminaire safety, photovoltaic system performance, and road-lighting layout practice. Georgetown’s coastal environment also makes corrosion control a procurement item, so **hot-dip galvanizing** and serviceable external battery placement are not optional details; they are part of the operating envelope.

Georgetown’s latitude near **6.8°N** and the assumed **6.5 peak sun hours** support off-grid operation without trenching on suitable corridors. The system is therefore a practical fit where utility coordination, flood exposure, or road disruption would make conventional grid lighting slower to deploy.
